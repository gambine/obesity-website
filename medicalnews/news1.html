Sugary drink complacency warning

Published: 16 October 2015


The National Heart Foundation warns against complacency despite newly released data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) showing a slight decrease in the number of Australians consuming sweetened beverages.


The results from the Australian Health Survey and the 1995 results, show the proportion of people consuming sweetened beverages had decreased slightly from just under half (49%) in 1995 to 42 per cent (two in five Australians) in 2011–12.


However, the most alarming finding showed the average intake for males aged 14–18 years, the highest consumers of sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs), was equivalent to a startling 16 teaspoons, or 68 grams on the day prior to the interview, with the average intake overall equivalent to 13 teaspoons, or 54 grams.


The findings of the report provides rationale for the National Heart Foundation to reiterate its call for tough regulatory action to combat the overconsumption of harmful sugary drinks, including the exploration of taxation to a ban on the marketing of junk food advertising to children and young adults.


National Heart Foundation CEO, Mary Barry said the findings underscore the need for urgent and decisive action to combat the known health impacts associated with the regular consumption of SSBs.


“Though today’s statistics show a slight decrease in SSB consumption, this is not yet a victory,” said Ms Barry.


“We cannot ignore the fact that regular and excessive consumption of SSBs, particularly in children and young adults, is continuing to exacerbate Australia’s obesity epidemic and remains one of our greatest public health priorities.


“We know the consumption of one or two SSBs daily can lead to excess weight gain and a greater risk of developing cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes.


“The majority of Australians (85 per cent) believe an unhealthy diet is a major concern for our children’s health with 70 per cent fearing their children will live a shorter life due to unhealthy dietary habits.


“It is vital that Government policy and tough regulatory action echoes community concern over the role SSBs play in Australia’s growing obesity crisis.


“The Heart Foundation has long advocated for regulatory and policy changes that reinforce behaviour modification, and will continue to do so until the Government takes decisive action.”


The report also found that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples were more likely to consume sweetened beverages than non-Indigenous people (56 per cent compared with 42 per cent). Their median intake was also higher at 455 mLs or one and a half cans of sweetened beverages.


“While the prevalence of SSB consumption among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples is disturbing, the health benefits of reducing sugar-sweetened beverage consumption among populations - both Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander and non-Indigenous - is unequivocal,” said Ms Barry.


The National Heart Foundation will continue to work with Government and aligned public health organisations to develop and implement a comprehensive national obesity prevention strategy.
